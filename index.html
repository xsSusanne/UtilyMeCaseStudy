The challenge

You suspect the bill is wrong. You don’t have a week to find out.

Verifying a Nebenkostenabrechnung in Germany today takes a Mieterbund consultation, an appointment with a lawyer, or hours alone with a spreadsheet and the BetrKV. For most tenants none of those happen. The bill gets paid, the suspicion lingers, the year ends.

Research conducted in early 2026 — three qualitative interviews in Berlin and a survey of 38 German renters — made the gap visible. Tenants are engaged and informed; they suspect something is off. But the cost of verifying is so high that paying-and-hoping is the rational choice.

“I always feel like something is off, but I have neither the time nor the legal knowledge to actually check.”
Anna, 32 · Marketing manager, Berlin · Interview 2, 2026
Who we designed for

Three people. One shared friction.

Anna

Marketing manager · 32 · Berlin

Native German speaker, mobile-first, time-poor. Suspects her bill is wrong but won’t take a half-day off to visit Mieterbund. Lives on her phone — the audit has to happen on a 15-minute U-Bahn ride or it does not happen at all.

Markus

IT consultant · 58 · Hannover

Long-term tenant, Mieterbund member, methodical. Wants the calculation, not just the verdict. Will use his phone but expects desktop-quality detail. Tests UtilyMe against his own spreadsheet.

Priya

Software engineer · 28 · Munich

Expat, English working language. Loses confidence the moment a document is in German legalese. Needs UtilyMe to bridge the language gap, not just the legal one. Will recommend it to her entire expat network if it earns her trust once.

The approach

Separate the expertise from the urgency.

The expertise — BetrKV interpretation, mathematical apportionment, error-pattern recognition — lives in the system. The user supplies one thing: their bill. Everything else happens in under ninety seconds.

Upload · the first 30 seconds

The first thirty seconds belong to the user.

Anna takes a photo of her bill or uploads a PDF. The interface asks for nothing else. No account, no email, no questions about her landlord. The friction has to be near zero or the tab closes.

Audit · the hybrid stack

An LLM extracts. Deterministic math audits.

A language model parses the document and pulls out the structured data — square metres, apportionment keys, individual line items, prior payments. A separate deterministic engine — not the LLM — runs the arithmetic. The LLM is the parser; the math is the auditor. This split is the trust architecture, and it is the most important architectural decision in the project.

Verdict · the trust artefact

The math IS the trust artefact.

The verdict is never “trust me, this looks wrong.” It is “here is what your bill says, here is what the math returns, and here is the line that does not reconcile.” Every figure is auditable. Every figure links to the source paragraph in the BetrKV. Users are encouraged to open the calculation detail view — we measure that.

The non-negotiable

UtilyMe is a tool, not a lawyer.

Every verdict carries a BetrKV disclaimer reviewed by external counsel. The product helps a tenant decide whether to investigate further — it does not replace Mieterbund consultation, and it does not generate legal advice. This positioning is embedded in the Definition of Done, the Go/No-Go gates, and every line of public copy. It is not a process step. It is a product value.

How it was built

From a problem statement to a launch-ready plan in 14 weeks.

01

Discover

Three qualitative interviews, a 38-person survey, competitive analysis (Mieterbund, lawyers, DIY). Eight hypotheses written before the data was looked at. Every later scope decision traces back to this evidence.

02

Define

Three personas, full user journey, user flow, 8 epics and 26 user stories with Given/When/Then acceptance criteria, Scrum framework with DoR/DoD, story-point estimation, lean PRD, RICE prioritisation, Now-Next-Later roadmap (iterated through three versions).

03

Design, specify, ship-ready

Lovable prototype iterated through three rounds of usability testing, five KPIs with full specification (formulas, owners, cadence), Performance & Experimentation Framework, twelve-week release plan with Go/No-Go gates, and a ninety-day post-launch plan. Twenty-eight deliverables in total.

Measuring what matters

Five KPIs. Each one tied to a moment that matters.

Audit Completion Rate

≥ 60%

If users start an upload and abandon before the verdict, the entire trust thesis breaks. This is the headline KPI of O1 — verify, not pay‑and‑hope.

Median Time to Verdict

< 90s

The product promise is “in sixty seconds, on your phone.” Every second above 90 is a second Anna’s attention is somewhere else.

Trust Channel Acquisition

≥ 40%

Mieterbund + Verbraucherzentrale + Stiftung Warentest. Trust is the moat, and it has to come through trusted channels first.

Calculation Detail Open Rate

≥ 70%

If users do not open the math, they are not really trusting the verdict. This is the leading indicator that the trust artefact is working.

Post-Audit Trust Score

≥ 4.0/5

The lagging indicator. Users tell us directly whether they would act on the result. Floor: 3.5. Below that, the strategy is at risk.

What I learned

Trust is built by what you choose not to do.

The hardest decisions in the project were what to leave out. The team had clear capacity for a dispute-letter generator, a recurring annual reminder, a paid premium tier. Each one tested well in concept. Each one was deferred. Trust at MVP comes from doing one thing — the audit — exceptionally well, transparently, in sixty seconds.

The math is not a feature. It is the product. The single most important decision in the architecture was splitting the LLM and the deterministic arithmetic. The LLM extracts; the math audits. Users can verify every number against their own bill. That separation makes the verdict defensible, and a defensible verdict is the only verdict worth shipping in this category.

User research is not a discovery activity. It is the spine. Every prioritisation conversation came back to what Anna, Markus and Priya told us. Without their interviews, the team would have built a thorough product. With them, it built a useful one.
